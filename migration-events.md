# Migration Events

Reference data for all migration events displayed on the Human Migration Map. Each event includes the route path, event markers, and drawer content.

---

## 1. Origin of Anatomically Modern Humans in Africa

- **Date:** ~200 kya
- **Location:** East Africa (Ethiopia)
- **Route:** None (origin point marker only)
- **Marker coordinates:** Omo Kibish, Ethiopia (~5.4°N, 35.9°E)

### Drawer Content

**Title:** Origin of Anatomically Modern Humans
**Date:** ~200,000 years ago

The oldest anatomically modern human (AMH) fossils date to ~150-190 kya in Ethiopia. Africa holds the highest genetic diversity of any continent, reflecting our species' long history on the continent. The deepest known split in the human family tree is between the San (click-language speakers) of southern Africa and all other human populations, dating to ~160-110 kya. At least 14 ancestral population clusters have been identified within Africa, showing extensive population substructure that predates any out-of-Africa migration.

## 2. Out of Africa

- **Date:** ~50-65 kya
- **Location:** East Africa → Middle East (Levant)
- **Route:** Ethiopia → across the Red Sea / Sinai → Levant
- **Route coordinates (approximate):**
  - Start: East Africa (~10°N, 40°E)
  - Via: Southern Arabian Peninsula (~15°N, 45°E)
  - End: Levant (~32°N, 35°E)

### Drawer Content

**Title:** Out of Africa
**Date:** ~50,000-65,000 years ago

A single major dispersal event carried a subset of African populations into the Middle East, founding all non-African populations alive today. Genetic diversity decreases steadily with distance from Africa, consistent with serial founder effects as small groups broke off and moved further from the origin. There may have been two early waves: one heading toward Oceania along the southern coastal route, and another into mainland Eurasia. All evidence — mtDNA phylogenies rooting in Africa, higher African genetic diversity, and ancient DNA — supports the Out-of-Africa model over the now-rejected multiregional hypothesis.

## 3. Neanderthal Admixture

- **Date:** ~50-65 kya
- **Location:** Middle East (Levant)
- **Route:** None (event marker at admixture location)
- **Marker coordinates:** Levant (~33°N, 36°E)

### Drawer Content

**Title:** Neanderthal Admixture
**Date:** ~50,000-65,000 years ago

Shortly after leaving Africa, modern humans interbred with Neanderthals in the Middle East. All non-African populations today carry ~2% Neanderthal DNA, with East Asians carrying ~20% more than Europeans — possibly due to additional admixture or dilution of Neanderthal ancestry in Europeans. The original admixture proportion may have been ~5-10%, reduced over time by purifying selection removing deleterious Neanderthal variants. Large "deserts" in the human genome are depleted of archaic sequence near functional regions. Some introgressed Neanderthal DNA was adaptive, particularly genes involved in immunity (MHC genes). Neanderthals may have been absorbed into the expanding human population rather than driven to extinction.

## 4. Settlement of Sahul (Australia & New Guinea)

- **Date:** ~47-55 kya
- **Location:** SE Asia → Sahul (Australia + New Guinea + Tasmania)
- **Route:** Levant → southern coastal route through South Asia → SE Asia → Sahul
- **Route coordinates (approximate):**
  - Start: Levant (~32°N, 35°E)
  - Via: Southern India (~10°N, 77°E)
  - Via: SE Asia / Sunda (~0°N, 105°E)
  - End: Northern Australia (~12°S, 132°E)

### Drawer Content

**Title:** Settlement of Sahul
**Date:** ~47,000-55,000 years ago

Sahul — the ancient landmass connecting Australia, New Guinea, and Tasmania during lower sea levels — was reached by a single founding event shortly after humans first left Africa. This makes Aboriginal Australians and Papuans descendants of one of the earliest migrations out of Africa. Aboriginal Australians remained in high genetic isolation until recent times. The Ust'-Ishim individual (~45 kya, western Siberia) shows equal genetic affinity to modern Western Eurasians, East Asians, and Aboriginal Australians, suggesting these populations had not yet fully diverged at that time.

## 5. Denisovan Admixture

- **Date:** ~45-50 kya
- **Location:** SE Asia / Oceania
- **Route:** None (event marker at admixture region)
- **Marker coordinates:** SE Asia (~5°S, 120°E)

### Drawer Content

**Title:** Denisovan Admixture
**Date:** ~45,000-50,000 years ago

Denisovans — known primarily from a finger bone and teeth found in Denisova Cave, Siberia — were the closest relatives of Neanderthals (diverged ~200-400 kya). Modern Melanesians carry ~3-6% Denisovan ancestry, while mainland Southeast Asians carry only ~0.1-0.3%. Denisovans themselves may carry DNA from even older hominins, possibly Homo erectus. Some introgressed Denisovan DNA proved highly adaptive: the EPAS1 gene, which helps Tibetans thrive at high altitude by producing fewer red blood cells in response to hypoxia (instead of the typical overproduction), was inherited from Denisovans.

## 6. Peopling of Europe

- **Date:** ~45 kya → ~4.5 kya (three major waves)
- **Location:** Middle East → Europe
- **Route:** Three overlapping routes into Europe
- **Route coordinates (approximate):**
  - Wave 1 (WHG): Levant (~32°N, 35°E) → Balkans (~42°N, 22°E) → Western Europe (~46°N, 2°E)
  - Wave 2 (EEF): Central Anatolia (~39°N, 33°E) → Balkans → Central/Western Europe
  - Wave 3 (Yamnaya): Pontic-Caspian Steppe (~48°N, 40°E) → Central/Northern Europe (~52°N, 13°E)

### Drawer Content

**Title:** Peopling of Europe
**Date:** ~45,000 years ago to ~4,500 years ago

Modern European ancestry derives from three major population waves. First, Western Hunter-Gatherers (WHG) recolonized Europe after the Last Glacial Maximum. Second, Early European Farmers (EEF) expanded from Central Anatolia ~11 kya during the Neolithic revolution, spreading agriculture by migration of people — not just ideas. The Neolithic lifestyle increased population sizes but sometimes led to poor health (malnutrition, dental caries). Third, Yamnaya steppe herders from the Pontic-Caspian region arrived ~4.5 kya on horseback, possibly spreading Indo-European languages. The Yamnaya themselves descended from hunter-gatherers of Russia and the Caucasus. Genetic diversity in Europe decreases with northern latitude, and Sardinians retain the most Neolithic (EEF) ancestry of any modern European population.

## 7. Crossing Beringia

- **Date:** ~23-15 kya
- **Location:** Siberia → Alaska (via Bering Land Bridge)
- **Route:** Northeast Siberia → Beringia → Alaska
- **Route coordinates (approximate):**
  - Start: NE Siberia (~63°N, 170°E)
  - Via: Beringia / Bering Strait (~65°N, -169°W)
  - End: Alaska (~63°N, -150°W)

### Drawer Content

**Title:** Crossing Beringia
**Date:** ~23,000-15,000 years ago

The Native American gene pool formed ~23 kya from the convergence of two ancestral branches: Ancient East Asians (the majority contributor, isolated ~36 kya) and Ancient North Siberians (e.g., Yana site, 31.6 kya; Mal'ta boy, 24 kya). These populations likely met during or just before the Last Glacial Maximum (~26-20 kya). The ancestral population walked from Siberia to Alaska via the Bering Land Bridge (Beringia), which may have served as a mild wetland refugium. Central Beringia (now underwater) is a candidate for where this population lived in isolation before expanding southward.

## 8. Peopling of the Americas

- **Date:** ~15-14 kya
- **Location:** Alaska → North and South America
- **Route:** Alaska → Pacific coastal route → South America
- **Route coordinates (approximate):**
  - Start: Alaska (~60°N, -145°W)
  - Via: Pacific Northwest coast (~48°N, -125°W)
  - Via: Central America (~15°N, -90°W)
  - End: Southern South America (~40°S, -70°W)

### Drawer Content

**Title:** Peopling of the Americas
**Date:** ~15,000-14,000 years ago

The oldest accepted archaeological evidence in the Americas dates to ~15-14 kya, with pre-Clovis sites including Monte Verde (Chile, 14.2 kya) and Page-Ladson (Florida, 14.45 kya). The ice-free corridor between the Laurentide and Cordilleran ice sheets was not viable until ~12.6 kya, meaning the first peoples must have traveled the Pacific coastal route. The Clovis-associated genome (~12.6 kya, Montana) is directly ancestral to contemporary Native Americans. Kennewick Man (~9.5 kya) was shown by genetics to be most closely related to Native Americans — not Ainu or Polynesians as cranial morphology had suggested. A basal north-south population split occurred ~14-13 kya. Native American ancestry is a mixture of East Asian and Ancient North Siberian (Mal'ta-like) lineages (14-38%).

## 9. Polynesian Expansion

- **Date:** ~800-1200 CE
- **Location:** Samoa → across the Pacific
- **Route:** Samoa → Cook Islands → Society Islands → Tuamotu → Marquesas / Rapa Nui
- **Route coordinates (approximate):**
  - Start: Samoa (~13.8°S, -171.8°W)
  - Via: Cook Islands / Rarotonga (~21.2°S, -159.8°W)
  - Via: Society Islands / Tahiti (~17.7°S, -149.4°W)
  - Via: Tuamotu Archipelago (~16°S, -145°W)
  - End (north): Marquesas (~9.8°S, -139.5°W)
  - End (east): Rapa Nui / Easter Island (~27.1°S, -109.3°W)

### Drawer Content

**Title:** Polynesian Expansion
**Date:** ~800-1200 CE

Polynesian seafarers expanded eastward across the Pacific from Samoa through a series of island-hopping migrations, each founded by small groups of 30-200 individuals. These serial bottlenecks caused genetic diversity to decline at each step, with rare variants lost or fixed by chance — a pattern used by Ioannidis et al. (2021) to reconstruct the settlement sequence. The Tuamotu Archipelago — low-lying atolls with few archaeological remains — emerged as a key expansion hub. From Tuamotu, settlers colonized the Marquesas (north), Raivavae (south), and Rapa Nui (east). These distant islands share a megalithic statue culture, now supported by their shared genetic ancestry. Genetic evidence also suggests Polynesian-Native American contact around ~1200 CE, possibly in the South Marquesas. Polynesians carry mixed Melanesian and East Asian ancestry, rooted in the Austronesian expansion from coastal southern China.
